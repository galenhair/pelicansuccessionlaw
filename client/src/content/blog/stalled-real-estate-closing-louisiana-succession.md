## Why Real Estate Closings Stall After a Death

When a Louisiana property owner dies, their ownership interest does not automatically transfer to their heirs. Until a **Judgment of Possession** is obtained and recorded in the parish conveyance records, the title is legally clouded — and no title company will insure it.

This means that if a family is trying to sell inherited property, or if a buyer has a property under contract and discovers that a previous owner's succession was never completed, the closing will stall until the succession is resolved.

## The Two Most Common Scenarios

### Scenario 1: The Family Is Selling Inherited Property

A parent or grandparent dies and leaves real estate to their children. The children want to sell. The title company runs a title search and discovers that the succession was never opened — or was opened but the Judgment of Possession was never recorded. The closing cannot proceed.

### Scenario 2: A Prior Owner's Succession Was Never Completed

A buyer is purchasing property and the title search reveals that a previous owner in the chain of title died without completing a succession. The current seller may have no idea the problem exists. The closing stalls until the old succession is opened and a Judgment of Possession is obtained and recorded.

## What We Need to Get Started

To open a succession for a stalled real estate closing, we need:

1. Certified death certificate of the deceased property owner
2. The deed showing the deceased's ownership interest
3. Names and contact information for all heirs
4. The original will, if one exists
5. The closing date (so we can prioritize accordingly)

## What Happens After the Judgment of Possession?

Once the Judgment of Possession is signed by the judge, we record it in the conveyance records of the parish where the property is located. The title company can then confirm clear title and the closing can proceed.
