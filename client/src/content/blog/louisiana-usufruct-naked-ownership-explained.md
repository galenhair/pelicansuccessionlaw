## What Is Usufruct?

**Usufruct** is a Louisiana legal concept that gives a person the right to use and enjoy property owned by someone else — including the right to collect rents and revenues — without owning the property itself. The person who holds the usufruct is called the **usufructuary**.

In the context of Louisiana successions, usufruct most commonly arises when a married person dies without a will. Under Louisiana's default intestate succession rules, the **surviving spouse receives a usufruct** over the deceased spouse's half of the community property for the duration of the surviving spouse's life.

## What Is Naked Ownership?

**Naked ownership** is the counterpart to usufruct. The naked owner owns the property but cannot use it, occupy it, or collect income from it while the usufruct is in place. In a typical Louisiana succession, the **children of the deceased receive naked ownership** of the deceased parent's half of the community property.

The children own the property — but the surviving parent has the right to live in the house, collect rent from it, or use it as they see fit until the usufruct ends.

## When Does the Usufruct End?

The usufruct ends when:

1. **The usufructuary dies** — at which point full ownership consolidates in the naked owners (the children)
2. **The usufructuary remarries** — under Louisiana law, the legal usufruct terminates automatically upon remarriage (unless the will provides otherwise)
3. **The usufructuary renounces the usufruct** — the surviving spouse can choose to waive the usufruct

## How Does Usufruct Affect Real Estate?

Usufruct has significant practical implications for real estate:

- The **naked owners cannot sell the property** without the usufructuary's consent
- The **usufructuary cannot sell the property** without the naked owners' consent
- Both parties must agree to any sale, refinancing, or major encumbrance
- The usufructuary is responsible for ordinary maintenance and property taxes

This means that if a surviving parent and adult children disagree about selling the family home, neither side can force a sale without the other's cooperation — unless they go to court.

## Usufruct in the Judgment of Possession

Every Louisiana succession involving community property and a surviving spouse must address usufruct in the **Judgment of Possession**. The judgment will typically:

1. Confirm the surviving spouse's ownership of their half of the community property (outright, with no usufruct)
2. Confirm the surviving spouse's usufruct over the deceased's half
3. Confirm the children's naked ownership of the deceased's half

Getting this language right is critical. An improperly drafted Judgment of Possession can create title problems that take years to resolve.

## Can a Will Change the Usufruct Rules?

Yes. A testator can expand, restrict, or eliminate the usufruct in their will. For example, a will can:

- Grant the surviving spouse a usufruct over the entire estate (not just the community half)
- Limit the usufruct to specific property
- Eliminate the usufruct entirely and leave the property outright to the children
