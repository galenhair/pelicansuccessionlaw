## What Is a Louisiana Succession?

A Louisiana succession is the legal process of transferring a deceased person's assets to their heirs. Because Louisiana operates under a civil law system — the only state in the country to do so — the process is fundamentally different from probate in other states.

The end result of a Louisiana succession is a **Judgment of Possession** — a court order that legally transfers ownership of the decedent's assets to the heirs. Banks, title companies, and government agencies require this document before they will transfer accounts, release real estate, or update vehicle titles.

## When Is a Succession Required?

Not every death requires a formal succession. Louisiana law provides a simplified alternative — the **Small Succession Affidavit** — for estates that meet all of the following criteria:

- The total value of the estate is $125,000 or less
- The estate does not include immovable property (real estate)
- At least 30 days have passed since the date of death

If the estate includes real estate, or if the total value exceeds $125,000, a formal succession is required.

## The Seven Steps to Opening a Louisiana Succession

### Step 1: Obtain the Certified Death Certificate

The first document you need is a **certified copy of the death certificate** from the Louisiana Vital Records Registry. You can order certified copies online through the Louisiana Department of Health or in person at the Vital Records office in New Orleans.

### Step 2: Determine Whether a Will Exists

Search the decedent's personal papers, safe deposit box, and attorney's files. In Louisiana, wills can also be registered with the **Louisiana Notarial Archives** in New Orleans or with the notary who prepared the will.

### Step 3: Identify and Value the Assets

Create a complete inventory of all assets — real estate, bank accounts, investment accounts, vehicles, mineral interests, and personal property. For real estate, obtain the current assessed value from the parish assessor's office.

### Step 4: Identify All Heirs

Identify every person with a legal claim to the estate. This includes forced heirs (children under 24 or permanently incapacitated), the surviving spouse, and any other heirs named in the will or entitled under intestate succession law.

### Step 5: Prepare the Succession Petition

An attorney prepares the **Petition for Possession**, which is filed with the district court in the parish where the decedent was domiciled at the time of death. The petition identifies the decedent, the heirs, and the assets.

### Step 6: File with the Parish Court

The petition is filed with the clerk of court, along with the death certificate, the will (if any), and the filing fee (typically $150–$350 depending on the parish).

### Step 7: Obtain the Judgment of Possession

The judge signs the **Judgment of Possession**, which is then recorded in the conveyance records of every parish where the decedent owned real estate. This is the document you will present to banks, title companies, and other institutions.
