## Testate vs. Intestate: The Basic Distinction

A **testate succession** is one where the deceased left a valid will. An **intestate succession** is one where the deceased died without a will — or with a will that is invalid under Louisiana law.

The distinction matters because it determines who inherits, in what proportions, and what legal steps are required.

## Testate Succession: When There Is a Will

### What Makes a Louisiana Will Valid?

Louisiana recognizes two types of valid wills:

1. **Olographic will**: Entirely handwritten, dated, and signed by the testator. No witnesses or notary required.
2. **Notarial will**: Typed or printed, signed by the testator in front of a notary and two witnesses.

A will that does not meet these requirements is invalid under Louisiana law — even if it was valid in another state where the testator previously lived.

### What Happens in a Testate Succession?

1. The will is filed with the court and probated (confirmed as valid)
2. The estate is distributed according to the will's terms — subject to forced heirship limitations
3. The attorney prepares the Petition for Possession incorporating the will's distribution

### The Forced Heirship Limitation

Even with a valid will, a testator **cannot disinherit a forced heir** (a child under 24 or permanently incapacitated) without legal cause. If the will attempts to do so, the forced heir can challenge it.

## Intestate Succession: When There Is No Will

When a person dies without a valid will, Louisiana's intestate succession laws determine who inherits. The rules depend on the deceased's family situation:

### Surviving Spouse and Children

- The **children inherit the deceased's separate property** outright
- The **children inherit naked ownership** of the deceased's half of community property
- The **surviving spouse receives a usufruct** (right of use) over the deceased's half of community property for life or until remarriage

### Children Only (No Surviving Spouse)

- The children inherit everything in equal shares

### Surviving Spouse Only (No Children)

- The surviving spouse inherits the deceased's half of community property outright
- Separate property passes to the deceased's parents or siblings

### No Spouse, No Children

Louisiana's intestate rules create a priority order: parents, siblings, grandparents, and more distant relatives.

## Which Is More Complicated?

Neither is inherently more complicated — both testate and intestate successions require the same core steps (petition, descriptive list, judgment). However:

- **Testate successions** require will probate and forced heirship analysis
- **Intestate successions** require careful heir identification and community property analysis
