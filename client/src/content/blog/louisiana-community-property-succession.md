## Louisiana Is a Community Property State

Louisiana is one of nine community property states in the United States. Under Louisiana law, most property acquired by either spouse during the marriage is **community property** — owned equally (50/50) by both spouses.

This has profound implications for successions. When a married Louisiana resident dies, only **their half of the community property** passes through the succession. The surviving spouse already owns their half outright and it does not pass through the succession at all.

## What Is Community Property?

Community property includes:

- **Wages and salaries** earned by either spouse during the marriage
- **Property purchased** with community funds during the marriage
- **Retirement accounts and pensions** accumulated during the marriage
- **Businesses** started or grown during the marriage using community funds
- **Debts** incurred during the marriage for community purposes

## What Is Separate Property?

Separate property includes:

- Property **owned before the marriage**
- Property **inherited** by one spouse (even during the marriage)
- Property **received as a gift** to one spouse individually
- **Damages** received for personal injury (though lost wages during marriage are community)
- Property **designated as separate** in a matrimonial agreement (prenuptial agreement)

## Why the Distinction Matters in a Succession

The community/separate distinction determines what goes into the succession:

| Property Type | Who Owns It at Death | What Passes Through Succession |
|---|---|---|
| Community property | 50% each spouse | Deceased's 50% only |
| Separate property | 100% deceased | 100% passes through succession |

A common mistake is assuming that everything the couple owned together passes through the succession. In fact, the surviving spouse's half of community property is already theirs — it does not need to be transferred through the succession.

## The Surviving Spouse's Rights to Community Property

When a married person dies intestate (without a will), the surviving spouse does not inherit the deceased's half of the community property outright. Instead:

- **Children receive naked ownership** of the deceased's half
- **Surviving spouse receives a usufruct** (right of use) over the deceased's half for life or until remarriage

This means the surviving spouse can continue to live in the family home and use the community assets — but the children are the true owners.

## Tracing Separate Property

One of the most complex issues in Louisiana successions is **tracing separate property** — proving that an asset is separate rather than community. If a spouse used separate funds to purchase property during the marriage, that property may be separate — but proving it requires documentation of the source of funds.

Without proper documentation, Louisiana law presumes that property acquired during the marriage is community property.
